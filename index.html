Do you ever look at this page and feel really annoyed?

No? No that was just me? Well, OK.

So. This really annoys me because it's not really helpful to a researcher or someone who wants to maybe sift through the documents and get a bigger grasp of what's been learned from them. There's no search, no filters, no path back to the original article citing this document. How does anyone figure out what a document actually proves?

I scraped a bunch of articles on The Intercept for links to documents hosted on their Documents page. Then I scraped some of the meta tags on those stories to get brief descriptions about them. It isn't every single document because I didn't use a complete list of every Intercept article ever. This is a thing to work on, I know. Please help if you want to help!

Other news websites that have published Snowden documents have done a somewhat better job contextualizing their releases—like look how The Guardian does it! And look at the work the ACLU is doing making Snowden documents searchable! This is not impossible, and I think it's something The Intercept cares about doing at some point. I mean, this is a thing I probably wouldn't have even made if Josh Begley and Margot Williams over at The Intercept hadn't started compiling an index of released documents which gave me a csv of articles to use as a starting point. (Thanks Josh and Margot you are really cool.)

This project was partly to solve a problem for me and partly so I could learn some things. I totally did! More on how I actually made this in the repo.
